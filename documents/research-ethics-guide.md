## Research Ethics Guide

Ethical research is not optional — it is the foundation of trustworthy findings. This guide covers the ethical principles and practical procedures that must be followed when conducting user research through this pipeline.

### Core Principles

**1. Respect for Persons**

Participants are autonomous individuals with the right to make informed decisions about their involvement. This means:
- Providing clear, honest information about the research purpose, process, and use of findings
- Allowing participants to decline any question without pressure or consequence
- Respecting participants' time by staying within the agreed duration
- Never deceiving participants about the nature of the research

**2. Beneficence**

Research should benefit participants, their communities, or the broader user base. This means:
- Designing research that will genuinely inform product improvements
- Sharing findings back with participants when possible
- Avoiding research that serves only internal curiosity with no actionable intent
- Ensuring the burden on participants (time, cognitive effort) is proportionate to the expected benefit

**3. Justice**

The benefits and burdens of research should be distributed fairly. This means:
- Not over-researching the same participants while ignoring others
- Ensuring participant recruitment does not systematically exclude populations who would benefit from the research
- Providing fair compensation for participation
- Making findings available to those who contributed to them

### Informed Consent

Every research participant must provide informed consent before any data is collected. The consent process must cover:

**What to communicate:**
- Purpose of the research (in plain language)
- What participation involves (activities, duration, recording)
- How data will be used and stored
- Who will have access to the data
- How long data will be retained
- The participant's right to withdraw at any time without consequence
- Any risks or potential discomfort

**Format:**
- Written consent for formal studies (interviews, usability tests)
- Implied consent for anonymous surveys (with a clear statement at the survey start)
- Verbal consent is acceptable if recorded, but written is preferred

**Special considerations:**
- If the research involves participants under 18, parental or guardian consent is required
- If the research involves employees of the organisation, ensure participation is genuinely voluntary and not perceived as a workplace requirement
- If the research involves sensitive topics, provide advance notice so participants can make an informed decision

### Data Privacy and Handling

**Data minimisation:** Collect only the data you need. If demographic information is not required for the analysis, do not collect it.

**Anonymisation:**
- Replace participant names with IDs (P1, P2, etc.) in all analysis documents
- Remove or redact company names, job titles, and other identifying details from quotes
- Be aware that combinations of demographic data can re-identify individuals even without names
- Never include participant contact information in research reports

**Storage:**
- Store raw data (recordings, transcripts) separately from analysis documents
- Use encrypted storage for interview recordings
- Limit access to raw data to the research team only
- Delete recordings after transcription and verification unless explicitly consented to longer retention

**Retention:**
- Define a retention period before the study begins (typically 12-24 months)
- Delete raw data at the end of the retention period
- Anonymised analysis and insights may be retained indefinitely
- Document what was deleted and when

### Sensitive Topics

Some research touches on topics that may cause discomfort:
- Job dissatisfaction or workplace frustration
- Financial stress or pricing sensitivity
- Accessibility needs or disability
- Competitive tools (participants may worry about getting colleagues in trouble)

When researching sensitive topics:
- Warn participants in advance during recruitment
- Provide the option to skip questions
- Have a plan for distressed participants (pause, offer a break, stop if necessary)
- Do not probe aggressively on emotional topics
- Frame questions neutrally, not in a way that suggests a "correct" emotional response

### Compensation

**General guidelines:**
- Compensate all participants for their time, regardless of the quality of their responses
- Set compensation at a rate that reflects the time commitment and expertise required
- Typical rates: 30-minute interview (£30-50 gift card), 60-minute interview (£50-100 gift card), survey (£5-10 gift card or equivalent)
- Pay promptly — within 48 hours of participation
- Never make compensation contingent on specific answers or outcomes

**Avoiding coercion:**
- Compensation should be fair but not so high that participants feel unable to decline
- For internal research with employees, consider non-monetary incentives (extra break time, first access to new features)

### Reporting Ethics

- Never misrepresent findings. If the data does not support a desired conclusion, say so.
- Present disconfirming evidence alongside supporting evidence
- Clearly distinguish between findings (what the data says) and interpretations (what you think it means)
- Acknowledge limitations honestly in every research report
- Do not cherry-pick quotes that support a narrative while ignoring contradictory ones
